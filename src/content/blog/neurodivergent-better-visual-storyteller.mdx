I was thirty-two before anyone told me I had ADHD.

By that point I'd built a career, launched a company, lived in four countries, and developed a creative practice that felt nothing like the way other people described their work processes. I thought the difference was personality. I thought the chaos, the hyperfocus, the inability to work the way productivity books said I should — was just me.

It was. But there was a name for it. And more importantly, there was a framework that helped me understand why some of what felt like dysfunction was actually the source of my best work.

## Pattern recognition at scale

The thing ADHD gave me before I knew it had a name is a kind of hyperactive pattern recognition. My brain is constantly scanning for connections — between ideas, visual references, emotional tones, cultural signals. It doesn't wait to be asked. It never fully stops.

In most contexts, this is exhausting and socially disruptive. In creative work, it's an extraordinary advantage.

Visual storytelling, at its core, is about making connections that aren't obvious. It's about seeing that two apparently unrelated things belong together, that a particular visual language will resonate with a particular audience, that the unexpected image or structure will do the emotional work better than the expected one.

I do this constantly, without trying. What used to feel like a scattered, undisciplined mind turns out to be a connection-finding engine running at higher than normal speed.

## Hyperfocus as a creative weapon

ADHD has two modes that look like opposites but are actually the same thing.

There's the scattered mode — distracted, unfocused, incapable of staying on task. This is what most people associate with the diagnosis.

And then there's hyperfocus — a state of deep, almost involuntary concentration where the outside world disappears and the work consumes everything. Hours pass. The phone goes unanswered. The output that comes out the other side of this state is often the best I'll produce on any given project.

Hyperfocus is not a choice. It's triggered by genuine interest, by the right kind of problem, by work that feels like it matters. Which means the environments and projects that allow it are disproportionately important to my output quality.

The projects where I feel the most creatively alive are the ones where I enter hyperfocus naturally and often. The projects where I'm constantly fighting distraction are the ones where something's wrong — with the brief, with the fit, with the stakes.

I've learned to treat the absence of hyperfocus as a diagnostic signal, not just a productivity problem.

## Empathy by necessity

Here's something that surprised me when I started paying attention to it: people with ADHD often develop unusually strong empathy skills — not because it comes naturally, but because it's survival.

When your own brain is unpredictable, you become very good at reading the room. At tracking social dynamics. At understanding what other people are experiencing, because you've spent so long trying to navigate a world that wasn't designed for your mind.

For visual storytelling, empathy is everything. The work I do is always in service of an audience — a room of investors, a customer base, a team navigating change. Understanding how those people feel, what they fear, what they want, what would make them lean in or shut down — that's the foundation of everything.

My ADHD didn't give me empathy as a gift. It gave me the necessity to develop it. I'll take that.

## The permission I needed

What changed when I got the diagnosis wasn't my capability. It was my relationship with my own process.

I stopped trying to work the way I thought I was supposed to work — in order, in steps, in the approved sequence — and started working the way I actually work. Jumping around. Following the thread that felt alive. Trusting that the structure would emerge from the chaos if I gave it time.

The quality of the output improved significantly.

More importantly, I stopped spending creative energy fighting myself. The bandwidth that used to go into managing my own process started going into the work. And that's a meaningful shift — because the part of creative work that requires original thought is also the part that's most vulnerable to interference.

Give me the conditions to work the way my brain actually works and the results are different from anything I produce when I'm trying to be normal.

## What I'd tell anyone who's neurodivergent in a creative field

The system wasn't built for you. That's not a failure on your part; it's a description of the system. Most educational and professional structures are designed for linear thinkers who process information sequentially and perform best under predictable conditions.

That's not you. It's probably not most people doing their best creative work either.

The goal isn't to fix your process so it looks like everyone else's. The goal is to understand your process well enough to build the conditions that make it work.

For me, those conditions include: uninterrupted blocks of time, genuine stakes, high creative autonomy, and clients who care about outcomes rather than process.

In those conditions, the brain that used to feel like a liability becomes the thing I'd least want to trade.
