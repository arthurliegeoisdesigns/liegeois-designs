I spent a significant portion of my twenties succeeding at things I hadn't chosen.

The right company, the right title, the right country, the right lifestyle signals. I was doing well by every measure that had been handed to me as relevant. I was also, somewhere underneath all of it, wondering when I was going to start feeling the thing I'd assumed success would feel like.

It took longer than it should have to understand that the feeling wasn't delayed — it was absent. Because the trophies I was collecting weren't mine.

## Borrowed metrics

Success is always defined against a standard. The question is where the standard came from and whether you've ever actually interrogated it.

For most people, the standards arrive early and invisibly. What your parents valued. What your education rewarded. What your peer group tracked. What the culture you grew up in held up as evidence that a life was going well.

These inputs create a metric system. And metric systems, once internalised, run quietly in the background, evaluating your life without asking whether the metrics themselves are right for you.

The problem isn't that external standards are bad. Some of them are genuinely good calibrations — financial stability, professional accomplishment, meaningful relationships. The problem is when you're optimising for a metric you never chose and wondering why hitting it doesn't produce the feeling you expected.

## The tell

The tell — the signal that you might be running on borrowed metrics — is a specific feeling at the moment of achievement.

Not satisfaction. Not the simple completion feeling of finishing something. A more complex experience: something like relief mixed with the immediate appearance of a new, higher threshold. I got the promotion, and the promotion immediately became the baseline from which I needed to achieve the next thing. The version of success I was holding in front of me moved forward each time I caught up to it.

This isn't motivation. This is a treadmill. And the treadmill is well-designed — it looks exactly like ambition from the outside.

Real achievement toward goals you've actually chosen feels different. Not necessarily euphoric — I've found that my deepest satisfactions are quiet rather than loud. But complete in a way the treadmill achievements weren't. Present. Yours.

## The cost of the wrong trophy

When I look back at the years I spent optimising for someone else's success definition, the cost I feel most acutely isn't the time. It's the creative and professional energy I didn't apply to the things that turned out to actually matter to me.

You have a finite amount of motivated effort in any given period of your life. Applied toward the right things, it compounds. Applied toward the wrong things, it produces results that don't transfer to anything you actually care about.

I was good at a version of professional life that I didn't want. That meant I had both the proof that I could do it and the evidence that doing it wasn't enough. Which is a more disorienting position than either having failed at it or never having tried it.

## What happens when you put the trophy down

The transition from borrowed success metrics to chosen ones is rarely clean.

Putting down the wrong trophy means admitting it was the wrong trophy — which means acknowledging the years spent collecting it were years spent pointing in the wrong direction. That admission has costs. Social costs, because the people who validated your path will have complicated reactions to your departure from it. Identity costs, because the version of yourself that was succeeding by those metrics is no longer the protagonist of the story.

What replaces it is initially less legible — both to you and to others. The work that feels like yours doesn't always come with obvious external validation. The metrics aren't standardised. You have to build them yourself, which requires first knowing what you actually value, which requires the kind of honest self-examination that the busy, trophy-collecting phase didn't leave much room for.

This is the uncomfortable middle. Most people pass through it on their way to something better. Some people panic and return to the legible metrics. Some stay in the middle longer than necessary, circling the same questions.

## What I know now

Success is not a position. It's an orientation — and it has to be oriented toward something you've actually chosen.

The choosing is harder than it sounds. Not because the choice is obscure — most people have some sense of what would feel like theirs — but because choosing requires giving up the trophies you haven't won yet in the existing game. And those uncollected trophies have a strange psychological pull, even when you know you don't actually want them.

The measure I try to apply now is different from the one I inherited. Not: what did I achieve? But: what did I build that I'm glad exists? What problems did I help solve that were worth solving? What work did I do that felt like mine?

These questions don't have standardised answers. They don't appear on anyone's ranking of successful people. They don't make great dinner party answers.

They're also the only questions, I've found, where the answers actually satisfy.

The borrowed trophy looks good on the shelf. But it was never going to change how you feel about the room.
