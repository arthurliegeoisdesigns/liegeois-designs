Instagram will tell you it takes three skills and an afternoon.

It doesn't.

I built this website — the one you're reading this on — with AI. Then I rebuilt it, with a newer model, into the version you're looking at now. Two builds, three different AI models, a Terminal window I kept open for weeks, and a learning curve nobody warned me about.

This is the full-disclosure version, including which models did what, where they differ, and where every one of them needed a human in the room.

## Starting from zero

I am a designer, not a developer. When the first build started, I had never pushed a commit, never opened GitHub Desktop, and treated the Terminal like a room in my house I didn't have keys to. I didn't know AI models had names, let alone that the choice between them mattered.

I know now. That knowledge cost me real money. More on that below.

## Build one: Opus, then Sonnet

The first version of this site was built with Claude Opus and Claude Sonnet 4.6, working through Claude Cowork — an AI workspace that reads and writes the actual files in your project, runs commands, and keeps context across sessions.

Here's the embarrassing, useful truth about that build: I ran Opus — the most powerful, most expensive model in the lineup — for weeks, on tasks that didn't need it. Renaming files. Writing alt text. Routine fixes. When I finally tried Sonnet for that class of work, the results were essentially identical and the cost wasn't. Nobody tells you this either: model selection is a budget decision, and the default is rarely the right one.

What both models did well: execute. Components, animations, deployment pipelines, a working blog — built in weeks, not months, without a developer on retainer. What both did less well: push back. They built what I asked for, quickly and competently, even when what I asked for wasn't the best idea available. The judgment stayed my job.

The bugs were real, too — vanishing logos traced to one line in a config file, twenty broken portfolio images, hours of debugging that no highlight reel will ever show you.

## Build two: Fable

Months later I ran a full redesign with Claude Fable, and the difference wasn't speed. It was thoroughness.

The redesign started with Fable auditing my existing site — every component, every file — and delivering thirty-six specific criticisms, several of them brutal. My accent color, it turned out, was literally set to black in the code. An accent the color of the background. It found that.

The working pattern changed, too. Where the earlier models executed my instructions, Fable ran a process: decision gates before build phases, trial fonts wired behind a keyboard toggle so I could compare two typefaces at full scale on my real homepage before buying either, three complete iterations on how sections flow into each other — because the first two worked technically and still felt wrong, and it rebuilt rather than defended them.

Twice, a deep bug had the same invisible root cause — a single CSS property on a wrapper element quietly breaking everything pinned to the screen. Fable found it, fixed it, then wrote a warning into the code so no future session could reintroduce it. It migrated 229 images off a CDN I didn't control with a script I could rerun safely. When I said I wasn't a developer and needed instructions like a recipe, every terminal command after that came numbered, with what success should look like.

And when I asked whether to buy a four-font bundle, it read the foundry's licensing terms and told me the bundle was wrong for my use — two individual styles were enough. It argued me out of spending money. That was new.

The honest caveat: thoroughness costs attention. Fable asks you to make real decisions, and there were moments I just wanted the thing built. If the earlier models were a fast studio team, this was a senior creative partner — and senior partners make you show up to the meeting.

## What three models taught me about the work

The models differ. The job doesn't.

Every build, on every model, needed the same things from me: a clear brief, taste, and the willingness to say "technically correct, but wrong." The deck-stacking scroll effect Fable built me was executed flawlessly — and I killed it, because it didn't feel like my studio. The model's response was to ask better questions and build the next version. That loop — direction, execution, judgment, again — is the actual craft. The AI compresses everything except the judgment.

So no, I don't think this replaces designers. I think it removes every excuse a designer ever had for not shipping.

## The honest verdict

If you're a non-developer, this will be harder than you've been told. There is a curve. You will sit with error messages. Budget for the learning, not just the subscription.

If you're a designer with a clear vision: the gap between what you can imagine and what you can ship has never been smaller. Pick the strongest model you can afford for the thinking, a cheaper one for the routine, and keep every decision that matters for yourself.
